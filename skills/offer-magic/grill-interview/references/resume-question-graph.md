# Resume Question Graph

Use this workflow to turn a resume, question bank, and real interview records into defensible preparation without treating a small sample as universal truth.

## Grade the sources

Keep observations and inference separate:

1. A resume paired with its actual interview record proves that particular interviewer followed those question paths.
2. Repeated questions across independent records support a reusable pattern, especially when they attach to the same kind of resume hook.
3. A broad question bank is a coverage and frequency hypothesis, not proof that a target interviewer will ask every item.
4. A third-party resume that received interviews is a viable screening example, not proof that its wording, layout, or any one bullet caused the pass.
5. Candidate answers and known outcomes are diagnostic traces, not model answers. An unsuccessful or unknown outcome does not identify a single causal mistake.

Never copy another candidate's claims, numbers, ownership, or answer content into the user's preparation.

## Build the graph

For each observed question, record:

| Source | Exact resume hook | Opening question | Follow-up chain | Question type and capability tested | Answer gap or interruption | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Group rows by hook type rather than company name. Typical hook types include architecture, named mechanism, metric, failure claim, evaluation method, product positioning, and broad skill claim.

Promote a pattern only when it repeats across sources or follows directly from a high-signal resume claim. Keep company- or candidate-specific branches labeled as examples. Diagnose whether the answer addressed that capability, not merely whether it contained correct related facts.

## Defend each bullet

Treat each important bullet as a thesis. Prepare a direct 10-second answer plus an evidence drawer with:

- problem, user, and constraint;
- personal responsibility versus project outcome;
- design choice, rejected alternative, and reason;
- concrete mechanism or artifact;
- failure, anomaly, or debugging path;
- validation, baseline or target gap, and what the evidence cannot prove;
- learning, next change, or transfer to a new scenario.

Use only drawers supported by evidence; an empty drawer is a preparation gap, not an invitation to invent detail.

The resume needs the thesis and enough proof to make it credible—often one mechanism and one validation boundary. These may naturally occupy one to two visual lines. Keep secondary alternatives, implementation detail, failure paths, limits, and learning in this drawer unless one is the actual differentiator. Flag a bullet as overloaded when two independent questions would naturally start from different clauses, not merely because the supporting evidence is specific; recommend removing or deferring the weaker clause instead of making the resume an exhaustive record.

Grade readiness:

- **green**: the thesis, ownership, mechanism, validation boundary, and at least one limitation or learning are defensible;
- **yellow**: the claim is true, but a likely material follow-up lacks evidence or a crisp explanation;
- **red**: the wording overstates ownership, result, scale, or a mechanism the candidate cannot explain.

Send red hooks to `grill-resume` for correction. Grill the user one material fact at a time for yellow hooks. Save green hooks in the project defense.

## Make the answer interruption-safe

Keep the three-minute project spine in the same order as the resume, but make each bullet independently enterable:

1. open with project problem, user, role, and outcome boundary;
2. state the first bullet thesis, then expose only its strongest evidence;
3. pause naturally before the next thesis so an interruption does not destroy the logic;
4. answer a follow-up directly, then return by naming the next resume thesis;
5. close with remaining gap and learning.

Evaluate readiness through branching follow-ups, not a memorized uninterrupted speech.
